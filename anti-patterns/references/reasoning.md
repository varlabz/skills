# Reasoning and Logical Fallacies

## Reasoning & Logical Fallacies

Avoid logical errors:

| Anti-Pattern | Fix |
|-------------|-----|
| False dichotomy — presenting only two options when more exist | List the actual range of options |
| Circular reasoning — using the conclusion as a premise | Provide independent evidence |
| Straw man — misrepresenting an argument to make it easier to attack | Address the strongest version of the counterargument |
| Appeal to authority — citing an expert outside their field | Cite experts in the relevant domain |
| Hasty generalization — drawing a broad conclusion from a small sample | Use adequate sample sizes or acknowledge the limitation |
| Correlation vs. causation — assuming A causes B because they're correlated | Show the causal mechanism |
| Survivorship bias — focusing on successes while ignoring failures | Include the full dataset, including failures |
| Non sequitur — a conclusion that doesn't follow from the premises | Ensure the conclusion follows from the evidence |

## False Balance

Don't present equal weight to unequal evidence:

| Anti-Pattern | Fix |
|-------------|-----|
| "Some say X, others say Y" (when evidence strongly favors one) | State what the evidence shows |
| "On the one hand... on the other hand" (when one side is weak) | Acknowledge the counterargument, then dismiss it |
| "It depends on your perspective" (when facts exist) | State the facts |
| "Both approaches have merit" (when one is clearly worse) | Say which and why |
